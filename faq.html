Where are the wild horses?

The largest population of wild mustangs and burros are found in Western U.S.; Arizona, California, Colorado,
Idaho, Montana, Nevada, New Mexico, Oregon, Utah, and Wyoming. Small populations reside in B.C. Canada, as well as
other U.S. states, such as Washington- these mustangs are not regulated by the BLM or USFS (more infor on this can be
found in the third question/answer down below). Approximately 33,000 mustangs and 4,700 burros call America's public lands
home since Feb. of 2010. More than half of all the wild mustangs are found in the state of Nevada.

What are "Herd Management Areas"?

Herd Management Areas (HMA) are designated boundaries where free-roaming mustangs are burros are known to live.
These lands were indentified upon the enactment of the Wild Free-Roaming Horse & Burro Protection Act of 1971. All HMA's are strictly managed by the BLM.
The U.S. Forest Services Territories has similarly set boundaries and managaes wild mustangs and burros as well. Horses and Burros
living on these managed lands are protected by the Act.

Free-roaming horses and burros living on U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service (USFWS) reserves, National Parks, State Parks,
private and county lands of Nevada, Indian/Native American Reservations are not managed by the BLM or the USFS,
nor are they protected by the Act.

Aren't Wild Mustangs and Burros overpopulated? And aren't they
overgrazing the public lands?

That's exactly what the BLM wnats people to believe. Here's what an actual biologist, Robert Bauer,
says on this matter.
"As a biologist that has documented the Calico Mt. HMA's many times, both previous to the 2009/2010
roundups, as well as during and after, these areas are not overpopulated as the BLM would like us to believe
rather they are very much underpopulated. The horses and burros need to be allowed to fill their niche in all
areas out west including the Calico Mts.. If allowed their numbers will stablize according to natures mandate"

What is the difference between American Wild Horses and Domestic Horses?

500 years of natural selection differentiates wild horses from domestic horses. However, mustangs
do descend from once-domesticated horses, but this mere fact does not negate their (currently unrecognized)
status as (a re-introduced) native species of North America. Morphologically, Wild Horses are hardier-thicker
bones, stronger legs and hooves, generally sound feet, yet they do range in appearance as they have had a lot of
genetic admixture - Spanish blood such as the Andalusion, as well as, the Barb and Jennet are among some of the
many influences in the creation of the American Mustang.

Are Mustangs an invasive species or a re-introduced Native species of
North America?

Short answer: mustangs are a re-introduced native horse species to North America.
